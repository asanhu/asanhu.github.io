I am always a fan of non-fiction, especially autobiographies or memoirs, as they allow me to "live" through a real person's life, experiencing their rise and fall, struggles, and everything that makes them who they are today.

However, this book is completely different from all the other memoirs I've read. It is a book about dying, about how one stays alive while feeling the last course of existence.

Staying alive in the face of dying seems to be a lesson that we deliberately ignore in our death-avoidant culture. Dying in one's 30s is unusual, but dying itself is not. Paul Kalanithi narrates his last years of life in such a gasping, yet peaceful way. He faced each stage of his illness with grace and dignity, not with a misguided faith that he would beat cancer, but with authenticity and sincerity to grieve the loss of the future he planned and to forge a new one.

"The physician's duty is not to stave off death or return patients to their old lives, but to take into our arms a patient and family whose lives have disintegrated and work until they can stand back up and face, and make sense of, their own existence." Paul practices this duty by narrating his life, re-examining his life to reflect on what he truly wants to do that is worth the last days of his life.

The epilogue written by his wife Lucy strikes my heart the most. She digests the striking contradiction of their newborn baby and dying husband at the same time. She struggles, fears, and finally accepts and braves the death of her love. She continues to feel such love and gratitude alongside the terrible sorrow the same way after Paul is gone. It feels very desperate and heartbroken when I read the epilogue, but it is also a beautiful narrative to read as you see them form a deeper connection and deeper meaning of each other's lives.

"What happened to Paul was tragic, but he was not a tragedy."

Note: this book is my travel buddy: bought in a bookstore in London, read it on the flight from London to Shanghai, and from Shanghai to New York, and finally finishes it on the train to D.C. Who would've thought I could start my weekend trip with such a sorrow 🙁
