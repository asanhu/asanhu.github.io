After three months and 500+ pages, I closed Babel this morning with the peculiar ache that comes from finishing something that has fundamentally shifted how you see the world. This was the longest fiction I've ever read, and Kuang earned every page.

Kuang poses a devastating question: what if translation was literal magic, and the British Empire weaponized it for colonization? At Oxford's Royal Institute of Translation, students create magic through silver bars by exploiting the untranslatable gaps between languages. The premise sounds fantastical, but the horror feels terrifyingly real when these translators realize they're being used as weapons against their own homelands.

"Translation in its essence, is always an act of betrayal," and Kuang makes this viscerally true.

The second half chronicles the inevitable rebellion. Watching Robin and his cohort discover their complicity in their own people's suffering is brutal. The way Kuang renders the Opium Wars through this fantasy lens made me think of those middle school history textbook photos—Canton residents destroyed by opium—but suddenly they felt immediate and personal rather than distant historical artifacts.

What's brilliant is how Kuang examines the seductive trap of tokenism. These characters exist on what Emma Dabiri calls the "top deck of the slave ship"—granted access to power within a fundamentally broken system. They simultaneously love and hate Oxford, craving belonging while recognizing their exploitation. Do you stay comfortable and avert your eyes, or do you destroy the machine?

The ending, where they choose to destroy both Babel Tower and themselves, reads less like defeat than recognition. Sometimes the only way to stop being complicit is to throw yourself into the gears of the system that shaped you.

This is dark academia with actual teeth—no romanticizing of elite institutions, just the uncomfortable truth about how power uses your identity against you while making you grateful for the privilege.
