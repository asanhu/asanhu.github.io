"Stay True" by Hua Hsu is probably one of the quickest memoirs I've read. Hsu, a Chinese American writer at the NY Times, surprised me. When I first saw the title "Stay True" and learned about the author's background, I assumed it would be another cheesy story of an immigrant child finding their identity. While it does touch on that theme, the book goes much deeper, evoking heartfelt emotions and provoking profound thoughts.

Most of the time, I forgot about Hsu's identity as I delved into his stories from a first-person perspective. He comes across as just an individual fortunate enough to discover his passion, surrounded by great friends, experiencing life's ups and downs, and being a self-proclaimed nerd who enjoys exploring old records while also being open to new genres. He deals with loss, like his friend Ken, and grapples with guilt, immersing himself in memories and eventually finding peace by documenting life's moments. He doesn't strive to assimilate forcefully, viewing assimilation not as a solution but as a challenge in itself.

At times, I found myself wondering which part of US society I would choose to fit into if given the chance to settle. However, I no longer dwell on endless identity struggles. I realize that I'm simply an individual with unique traits, not trying to conform but staying true to what brings me joy and excitement each day.

The immigrant culture portrayed in the book is appealing for its recognition of differences without making them mere novelties. It emphasizes finding or creating similarities based on shared interests, passions, and hopes for the future, rather than clinging to rigid cultural norms. This fluidity is what allows us to live authentically and build our own cultural identities.
