My third Kazuo Ishiguro book, and a fun read with mixed feelings. The story follows Christopher Banks, a reputed English detective navigating the French Concession in Shanghai on the eve of the Second World War, on a mission to find his parents who went missing under causes he believed to be noble, but turned out to be an idealized white lie to protect him all along.

What stayed with me is how interesting it is to view that traumatic history through the lens of an Englishman living in an ivory tower. Even as he wanders through the combat zone completely untouched, he remains profoundly disconnected from reality, witnessing a girl's entire family dying on the floor, yet still trying to convince Chinese victims that his Japanese soldier friend was innocent. It felt as though he never left the well-protected bubble of his International Settlement childhood, unable to truly confront the cruel reality unfolding right in front of him. How, within the same country and even the same city, people can live completely different lives based on their race.

There's something darkly ironic in how Ishiguro uses the detective genre as a vehicle for self-deception as Banks is celebrated as a great detective yet spends the whole novel failing to solve the most personal case of his own life.

I was also surprised to see Kazuo writing about this history in such a critical and subtly sarcastic manner as a Japanese-British author, which makes his perspective even more special. Now onto the next Kazuo Ishiguro book!
